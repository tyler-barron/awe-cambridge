Dimension 02

Morphology

What are the external forms or stimuli — the people, places, and things — that give rise to awe? Morphology describes the shape of what triggers the feeling.

Atlas filter: Awe type & Physical form →
15 physical forms — the immediate trigger
Animals
Birds and mammals encountered in the city — creatures whose wildness interrupts the built environment.
Architecture
Building forms, enclosure, color, materiality, and ornament — the felt presence of human craft at scale.
Art & Objects
Murals, sculptures, installations, found items, and museum exhibits — things made for looking at.
Communities
Social bodies, organizations, and collections of people with shared purpose — awe in the presence of collective belonging.
Events
Gatherings, performances, festivals, lectures, sporting events, celebrations — awe that only exists in the moment.
Green Space
Designed or semi-designed outdoor spaces — parks, gardens, and urban nature in its various states of cultivation.
Infrastructure
Bridges, alleyways, boathouses, and transit spaces — the city's structural skeleton made visible and felt.
Light
Sunsets, reflections, shade, and seasonal lighting changes — the city's most ephemeral material.
People
Strangers, individuals, historic figures, and the presence of others — awe at human presence itself.
Plants
Trees, lawns, flowers, shrubs, gardens, and mushrooms — the non-human life embedded in the city's fabric.
Smells
Olfactory encounters with food and natural elements — the most primal and least mapped of urban senses.
Sounds
Auditory encounters created through movement or nature — the city heard rather than seen.
Vistas
Expansive views often of the sky, cityscapes, and bodies of water — awe at spatial openness and distance.
Water
Rivers, canals, and ponds — the presence of a non-human system moving through the city.
Weather
Rain, snow, ice, frost, and seasonal transitions — the atmosphere as an active presence in daily life.
8 wonders of life — the deeper category

Drawn from Dacher Keltner's Awe, these eight archetypes describe the deepest sources of human awe — the universal categories beneath the specific physical forms.

🌿
Nature
Awe in the face of the living, non-human world — plants, animals, weather, and ecological systems.
🏛
Visual Design
Awe in the presence of beauty made by human hands — architecture, art, and the crafted environment.
🕯
Life & Death
Awe at the fragility and magnitude of existence — birth, aging, grief, and mortality made present.
🌊
Collective Effervescence
Awe in the shared energy of a crowd or community — the feeling of being swept up in something larger than yourself.
🎵
Music
Awe through sound organized in time — the way music moves through and beyond the body.
✨
Epiphany / Knowledge
Awe in a flash of understanding — the sudden expansion of one's frame of reference.
🧡
Moral Beauty
Awe at extraordinary goodness, virtue, or kindness — the recognition of human excellence.
🕊
Spirituality / Religion
Awe encountered in sacred spaces, rituals, and encounters with the transcendent or numinous.

Dimension 03

Phenomenology

What are the perceptions and defining qualities of the experience of awe? Phenomenology describes the inner texture of the feeling — how it registers in the body and mind.

Atlas filter: Characteristics, tensions & binaries →
5 characteristics — what the experience contains
Beauty
The perception of aesthetic harmony or sensory pleasure in the natural or built environment.
"In cities judged from photos to be more evocative of visual awe, people report more robust health." — Keltner
Mystery
The encounter with something that cannot easily be explained or understood.
"Awe requires the unexpected, moves us toward mystery and the unknown." — Keltner
Wonder
The state of curiosity, openness, and receptivity.
"Approach what you see with fresh eyes, imagining that you are seeing it for the first time." — Keltner
Vastness
The perception of something physically, temporally, conceptually, or socially large that transcends your current understanding.
"Awe is the feeling of being in the presence of something vast." — Keltner
Interconnection
The sense of being part of a larger whole — seeing yourself as embedded in a system or network.
"Awe shifts us to a systems view of life." — Keltner
6 tensions — the spectrum each experience sits on

Each instance of awe was located on one side of a tension — a pairing that captures the quality of vastness perceived. Drawn from six frames of reference: formal, social, ecological, evaluative, temporal, and spatial.

Ornate ←→ Utilitarian
Built forms that exceed their function through decoration, craft, and aesthetic excess — versus those that perform their function with elegance and fidelity. Aspects include: historical richness, material quality, and unnecessary beauty.
Does this thing do more than it needs to, or exactly what it needs to?
Inclusive ←→ Exclusive
Spaces and communities experienced as open and inviting — versus those experienced as inaccessible or closed, often generating awe through opacity and mystery. Aspects include: belonging, boundaries, and intrigue.
Does this space invite you in, or hold you at a boundary?
Wild ←→ Curated
Living things that exceed or escape human management, asserting their own autonomy — versus those defined by human care, cultivation, and maintenance. Aspects include: intentionality, ecological agency, and stewardship.
Is this place managing nature, or is nature managing itself?
Impressive ←→ Ordinary
Things and experiences that exceed standards or expectation for their category — versus those that pleasantly stand outside of normal categorization. Aspects include: scale, significance, expectation, and the everyday.
Does this thing exceed a normal range, or does it stand quietly outside of comparison?
Novel ←→ Familiar
Encountering something genuinely new, or a known thing made new — versus encountering something already known, seen again more deeply. Aspects include: time, memory, attentiveness, and a relationship to place.
Is something new being noticed, or is it being seen more deeply?
Expansive ←→ Intimate
Spaces that highlight the body's relationship toward something vast and outward — versus spaces that hold, enclose, or provide refuge. Aspects include: bodily orientation, spatial scale, and containment.
Does this space open outwardly beyond you, or does it help draw you in?
4 phenomenological binaries — qualities that are either present or absent

Unlike the tensions, these four qualities don't have an opposite that generates awe — they are simply present or absent in a given experience. Each can be used as a filter in the Atlas.

S
Surprise
The experience of awe was unanticipated or caused a redirection of attention. Aspects include: discovery and disruption of routine.
Was this moment of awe unexpected, or did it interrupt the moment?
A
Alterity
The experience involved an encounter with irreducible otherness — a recognition of presence beyond the self. Aspects include: receptivity and an animate other.
Did this moment help you recognize and appreciate something beyond you?
T
Threshold
The experience involved a felt sense of transition — movement across a spatial, sensory, or perceptual register. Aspects include: transformation, passage, and liminal spaces or moments.
Did this moment denote a passage from one quality to another?
S
Sanctuary
The experience was accompanied by a felt sense of enclosure and refuge. Aspects include: solitude, shelter, intimacy, and nooks or enclosures with sanctuary-like qualities.
Did this moment of awe feel like being held rather than being drawn outward?

Dimension 04

Temporality

What is the temporal nature of this experience of awe? Is it a singular event or part of a cycle? What is its potential for repetition — and can it be revisited?

Atlas filter: Frequency (default: Timeless) →
6 frequencies — how often this awe is available

Drawn from Henri Lefebvre's Rhythmanalysis, these categories situate each experience of awe in time — from the once-in-a-lifetime to the permanently present. The Awe Atlas defaults to Timeless so you're always looking for something that's available right now.

Ephemeral
Once — unlikely to occur again. Rarity and singularity.
Periodic
Unpredictable or inconsistent cycle. Irregularity and synchronicity.
Seasonal
Returns with natural cycles — migration, flowering, weather.
Recurring
Scheduled by human activity — calendar, programming, celebration.
Daily
Available within an ordinary day — diurnal rhythms, open hours.
Timeless
Continuously available — persistent, durable, always there.
Ephemeral
An instance of awe that occurred once and could not reliably be recreated. Reflects the rarity of certain alignments of circumstance.
Is this moment of awe likely to occur just once?
Periodic
Part of an unpredictable or inconsistent cycle. May happen again but on an unidentifiable or sporadic basis.
Does this moment return on a cycle that can't quite be predicted?
Seasonal
Dependent upon natural cycles — aligned with Lefebvre's notion of cyclical time, originating in the cosmos and nature.
Does this moment of awe return with the seasons?
Recurring
Available based on an anthropocentric timeframe — weekly, monthly, or yearly. Aligned with linear time derived from social practice.
Does this moment recur based on a calendar or human schedule?
Daily
Marked by everyday rhythms — available within diurnal time, such as the movement of the sun or a museum's regular hours.
Is this available within the rhythm of an ordinary day?
Timeless
Connected to physical elements in the built environment — continuously available regardless of season, schedule, or weather.
Is this awe available in any given moment?
Temporal binary — history as active presence
H
History
The experience of awe was accompanied by a felt sense of the past or an awareness of the accumulation of time. Aspects include: longevity, connection to certain time periods, and the presence of time itself. "History, in the sacred sense, is one that is still alive, one in which we can participate" (Sacred Experience and Place in Cities, Krasin, 1976).
Was this moment of awe informed by the past, or connected to something old?
