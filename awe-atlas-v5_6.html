What is Awe?

The science of wonder, and why it matters.

The definition

A feeling of vastness that transcends understanding

Awe is what we feel when we encounter something so vast — physically or conceptually — that it momentarily exceeds our ability to take it in. It stops us. It shifts our sense of scale. It reminds us that the world is bigger than our problems.

Psychologist Dacher Keltner of UC Berkeley has spent two decades studying awe. In surveys across 26 countries, he found that people experience it two to three times a week — far more often than we realize. The challenge isn't finding awe. It's learning to notice it.

Keltner's 8 wonders of life

Awe comes in eight forms

Every place in this atlas is tagged with one of these — and every walk you take is an invitation to find one.

✦
Moral Beauty
The most common source of awe worldwide. Witnessing courage, kindness, generosity, or someone overcoming. A stranger paying for someone's meal. A child caring for a grandparent.
🌿
Nature
Vast skies, deep water, ancient trees, the pattern of frost on glass. Nature doesn't need to be grand to inspire awe — it needs to feel bigger than you.
◯
Collective Effervescence
When a group of people attends to the same thing and moves together — a concert, a protest, dawn swimmers at a reservoir. The boundaries between individuals blur into a shared "we."
♪
Music
The goosebumps. The held breath. How grief and joy can travel through sound. Music's capacity to convey the ineffable is one of awe's great mysteries.
◑
Art & Visual Design
A mural that watches you. Light through a rose window. The proportion of a doorway. When design reaches beyond function into something that briefly stops time.
🕯️
Spirituality & Religion
The feeling of surrendering to something larger than yourself — whether inside a cathedral, in prayer, in meditation, or simply standing under a night sky.
⧖
Life & Death
The awe of emergence and ending. A birth. A last breath. The realization that everything is temporary. These are the moments that most reliably recalibrate what matters.
◇
Epiphany
A sudden shift in understanding — when a big idea lands and rearranges something in your thinking. The awe of realizing you were wrong, or that the world is stranger than you thought.
What awe does to you

Why it matters

Awe is not a luxury. Research shows it has measurable effects on the body and mind — effects that accumulate with practice.

↓
Reduces self-focus, anxiety, and ego-driven thinking
Keltner, UCSF 2020
↑
Increases compassion, generosity, and social connection
Sturm et al., 2022
↑
Expands your sense of time — you feel less rushed
Rudd, Vohs & Aaker
↓
Lowers inflammation and activates the vagus nerve
Stellar et al., 2015

In an eight-week UCSF study, participants who took weekly awe walks — compared to those who simply walked — reported greater joy, more compassion, less daily distress, and progressively bigger smiles in their self-portraits. The more awe walks they took, the more the benefits compounded.

The practice

What an awe walk actually is

An awe walk is simply a regular walk taken with a particular orientation — outward attention instead of inward rumination. It doesn't require a destination. It requires a stance.

01Leave your phone in your pocket. Move at a pace that lets you notice things.
02Approach what you see with fresh eyes — as if for the first time. Children do this naturally.
03Look for vastness. Not just grand scale — the detail of a leaf, the age of a wall, the sound of many voices.
04Go somewhere new when you can. Novelty is one of the most reliable conditions for awe.
05Let yourself be surprised. Awe doesn't announce itself. It interrupts.

Awe Atlas is built on this practice — a tool for finding the places where awe is most likely to find you.

About this project

A field guide to wonder

Most of us move through familiar places without really seeing them. Awe Atlas is an attempt to change that.

Are you in a new place and not sure where to go?

Are you tired of your usual route — the same blocks, the same nothing?

Do you want to step outside and feel something, but you don't know where to start?

What if wonder didn't require a ticket, a reservation, or a plan?

Awe Atlas was built on a simple observation: awe is everywhere, and most of us walk past it every day. The staircase garden nobody notices. The church that fills with light at noon. The strangers who swim together at dawn without ever exchanging names.

These places exist in every city. They don't appear on tourism maps. They live in the knowledge of people who wander — and they're only findable if someone bothers to write them down.

This app is part dérive — the Situationist practice of drifting through a city guided by its psychogeographic pull rather than habit or destination. Part StumbleUpon — the serendipitous discovery of something you didn't know you needed. And part field guide — a living, crowdsourced record of where awe lives in a city, written by the people who found it.

The compass wander mode is especially built for this: no address, no blue dot. Just a direction and a warmth signal, and the invitation to get slightly lost on purpose.

Research by psychologist Dacher Keltner and others has shown that awe — the feeling of encountering something vast and not immediately understood — reduces anxiety, increases compassion, expands your sense of time, and makes you feel part of something larger than yourself. It is one of the most powerful and least accessed emotional states in modern life.

An awe walk doesn't require a special destination. It requires a particular kind of attention: outward, unhurried, open to interruption. Awe Atlas tries to give that attention a direction.

Origin

This project began as a thesis at MIT, developed in partnership with research into the psychology of awe. The places in the atlas are real. The notes are written by people who visited them. The walks are organized by people who want to share what they found.

If something moves you — a place, a moment, a thing you noticed — you can add it. The atlas grows when people write things down.
